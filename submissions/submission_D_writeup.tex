\section{Problem framing \& assumptions violated}

\emph{Every assumption a credit scorecard leans on---random labels, population
overlap, a stationary window, honest inputs---breaks on this book; we name each break
and respond to it rather than modelling straight through it.}

We are re-underwriting a historical SMB loan book to maximize realized portfolio net
present value (NPV)---total dollars earned, not classification accuracy.

\textbf{Labels are missing-not-at-random behind a sharp gate.} Outcomes exist only for
loans the prior lender approved, and approval is, to numerical precision, a hard cutoff
$\code{prior\_underwriter\_score} \geq 0.273$: a propensity model separates approved from
declined with AUC $1.00$ and \emph{zero} score overlap. So the label is selected on a
deterministic function of an observed covariate---textbook sample-selection bias. Inverse-propensity
reweighting is infeasible here by a positivity violation, \emph{not by our choice}: a propensity
model on the ordinary covariates alone still hits AUC $1.00$, so approved and declined applicants
share no common feature support. Only local extrapolation near the cutoff is available, and we
widen intervals on declines rather than pretend we can reweight.

\textbf{The split is forward in time.} Training spans an 18-month book ending the day before
the 13-week scoring window opens, over which the default rate drifts $17.5\% \to 20.6\%$. We
learn default \emph{timing} on the history but recalibrate the \emph{level} in-window---the
history tells us the shape, only the window tells us the rate.

\textbf{Missingness is information, not absence.} ``Never declined elsewhere'' (null) loans
default at $14.3\%$ versus $21.3\%$ when present; no-bank-feed loans at $19.1\%$ versus
$16.7\%$---a blank field predicts the outcome, so we encode it as a first-class indicator
rather than impute it away. \textbf{Self-reports are optimistically biased}: applicants who
overstate revenue by more than $1.5\times$ default at $38.6\%$ versus $16.1\%$, so the
\emph{gap} between stated and bank-feed revenue---not the stated figure---is the signal,
central to our causal treatment of \code{stated\_annual\_revenue} below.

\section{Methodology}

\emph{One discrete-time model serves all three deliverables: a calibrated probability
that a loan defaults, times a per-band curve for \emph{when} it defaults, read out as an
NPV sign for the approve/decline call.}

\textbf{One shared discrete-time model.} The brief's NPV depends on the \emph{day} of
default (a day-5 default loses roughly the principal; a day-55 default nearly breaks
even), and Deliverable B is the cumulative-default curve. Both consume one timing
model. We write each loan's cumulative-default curve as
$F_i(t) = \PD_i \cdot S_{b(i)}(t)$, where $\PD_i = \Pr(y_i{=}1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i)$ is a
calibrated probability of default by day~90 and $S_{b}(t)$ is a normalized timing
shape ($S_b(90){=}1$) learned per credit band $b$. In words: \emph{how likely} a default
is, kept separate from \emph{when} it arrives.

\textbf{PD model.} A five-fold \code{GroupKFold} (by \code{business\_id}) LightGBM
ensemble over 55 engineered features (out-of-fold AUC $0.774$, Brier $0.117$),
isotonic-calibrated. Features are deliberately buffer-centric: because loans are
$\sim$1.3\% of annual revenue, default is cash-buffer bound rather than revenue bound, so
debt-service coverage, buffer-to-payment, overdrafts and credit utilization dominate.

\textbf{The timing shape is bimodal and we model it as such} (Figure~\ref{fig:traj}).
Of all defaults, $77.5\%$ are missed-draw events over days $3$--$60$; \emph{no} defaults
occur on days $61$--$89$; then the remaining $22.5\%$ spike exactly at day~90 (the
open-balance sweep). A smooth survival fit would smear that mass across weeks, so we
encode the empirical concave-rise/flat/day-90-cliff shape rather than blur the cliff. The shape is
band-conditional---worse-credit borrowers default $\sim$13 days earlier and carry less
day-90 mass---so $S_b(t)$ feeds both B and the per-loan $\E[t^*]$ used in A.

\textbf{Deliverable A: decide on the NPV sign.} We approve iff
$\E[\NPV_i \mid \text{approve}] > 0$ using the exact product economics: revenue
$F_i + R_i r (T/365)$ if repaid, and $F_i + D_i(t^*_i - 1) + \mathrm{rec}_i - R_i$ if
default occurs at day $t^*_i$. That is, we keep the fee plus interest on a loan that
repays, but only the fee plus the daily draws collected before $t^*$ (less principal,
plus $\approx 9\%$ recovery) on one that defaults---so a default costs less the later it
lands. Because NPV is linear in $t^*$, the expectation uses the band-conditional expected
default day. The break-even therefore sits at PD $\approx 0.39$, not $0.5$: even defaulters
repay $\sim$74\% of the schedule through daily ACH before defaulting, so a $39\%$-PD applicant
is still NPV-positive and a $0.5$ cutoff would leave money on the table. We approve 73\% of
applicants; on realized validation outcomes this book returns $1.74\times$ the prior
underwriter's P\&L.

\textbf{Deliverable B: shape $\times$ level.}
$\mathrm{CDR}_{w,a} = \mathrm{mean}_{i \in A_w}\, \PD_i \, S_{b(i)}(a)$---we average each
approved cohort $A_w$'s per-loan risk over the timing shape, giving a curve that only rises.
Because the PD model has no cohort signal (training predates the window), we shrink each
cohort's \emph{level} toward the realized validation rate for the same calendar week
(hierarchical-Bayes random effects; pseudo-count 75 = prior precision); validation and test
span the identical 13 weeks, so this transfers.

\section{Causal reasoning \& counterfactual methodology}

\emph{The judges' counterfactual queries ask what an intervention \emph{causes}, not what a
feature \emph{predicts}---so we classify every feature by whether editing it changes reality,
and scale the effect accordingly.}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.36\textwidth]{shap_summary.png}
\caption{SHAP summary for the PD model: mean $|$SHAP$|$ ranking with per-feature
direction (red high / blue low). High delinquency, utilization and volatility raise PD;
high debt-service coverage and cash buffer lower it.}
\label{fig:shap}
\end{figure}

The counterfactual target is
$p_q^{\mathrm{cf}} = \Pr\!\big(y_{i_q}{=}1 \mid \mathrm{do}(f_q{=}v_q),\,
\mathbf{X}_{i_q,-f_q}{=}\mathbf{x}_{i_q,-f_q}\big)$ versus the observational
$\Pr(y \mid f{=}v, \mathbf{X}_{-f})$: the first asks what happens if we \emph{set} $f$ to $v$,
the second describes applicants who happen to \emph{have} $f{=}v$, and they diverge exactly
when $f$ is confounded. Our \textbf{feature registry} keeps the edit honest: each engineered
feature declares its parents, so setting a raw feature recomputes exactly its downstream
features and nothing else---never an internally-contradictory applicant carrying a \$20k loan
with a \$10k loan's payments. For example $\mathrm{do}(\code{requested\_amount})$ recomputes the
daily-payment, buffer-to-payment, leverage and ask-to-revenue features; the provided
\code{requested\_amount\_to\_observed\_revenue} is never reused (no stale-descendant bug).

\textbf{We classify the intervenable features by causal status.} \emph{Mechanical causes}
(\code{requested\_amount}): changing the number changes the borrower's actual payment burden,
so we perturb and propagate it through the registry at full strength. \emph{Confounded
proxies}---bureau and bank-feed symptoms of latent health (utilization, delinquency, revenue
and cash signals)---are symptoms as much as causes: editing the thermometer does not cure the
fever, so we keep only the estimated causal fraction
$\hat\lambda = \beta_{\text{adj}}/\beta_{\text{naive}}$ (sibling-adjusted logistic; per-family
$\hat\lambda \approx 0.52,\,0.51,\,0.64$ for bureau, bank-feed, behavioral), averaged with the
heuristic and falling back where $\hat\lambda \notin (0,1)$. Conditioning sets are chosen \emph{empirically} (Figure~\ref{fig:proxy}): an
FDR-controlled sparse partial-correlation graph of the proxy block ($q{=}0.05$,
$n{=}55$k) confirms one dominant latent-health factor (32\% of variance, $2.3\times$ the
second) but flags ``siblings'' that are in fact \emph{descendants}---invoice delinquency
and overdrafts are consequences of low cash (partial corr.\ $-0.78$), payroll regularity
of revenue ($+0.50$). Conditioning on a descendant blocks the true causal path, so we
exclude them from those features' adjustment sets, correcting
$\hat\lambda_{\text{cash}}$ from an implausible $0.03$ (cash buffers mechanically fund
the daily draw) to $0.44$.
\emph{Self-reports}---\code{stated\_annual\_revenue}, \code{stated\_time\_in\_business}---are
returned at their observational PD: a different number on the form changes nothing about the
business. Overstating revenue \emph{predicts} default ($38.6\%$ vs $16.1\%$), but \emph{forcing}
a different statement causes nothing, so the interventional effect is $\approx 0$---formally,
$\Pr(y \mid \mathrm{do}(\text{stated}), \mathbf{X}_{-\text{stated}}) =
\Pr(y \mid \mathbf{X}_{-\text{stated}})$ when the claim itself does not cause default, the
causally correct answer rather than naive re-prediction. \emph{Immutable or proxy-by-fiat} features
(\code{sector}, \code{geography\_region}, \code{vintage\_years}, \code{has\_linked\_bank\_feed})
are not manipulable levers but are forced into the query set; we perturb the model, shrink the
effect, widen the interval, and flag them as proxy effects.

\textbf{Regulator defense.} Drivers are explained with SHAP over an inspectable,
de-correlated feature set (Figure~\ref{fig:shap}). The leading drivers---invoice-payment
delinquency, credit utilization, revenue volatility, requested amount, overdrafts, and the
affordability ratios---are all recognized credit or affordability signals moving in the
expected direction, and none is a prohibited or proxy-discriminatory attribute. Several queried
values lie outside observed support (e.g.\ \code{prior\_loans\_count} is in-support only 29\%
of the time); these receive wider intervals and an explicit extrapolation caveat.

\textbf{Model-class check.} A transparent logistic regression on the same engineered
features matches the gradient-boosted models (out-of-fold AUC $0.775$ vs $0.770$, equal
Brier) and is in fact \emph{more} robust to the train-to-window drift (validation AUC
$0.759$ vs $0.751$)---evidence the engineered space is near-linearly sufficient and the
drivers directly interpretable. Its standardized coefficients agree in sign with SHAP on
eight of the top ten features; the two exceptions (\code{buffer\_to\_payment},
\code{debt\_to\_revenue}) are collinearity-driven sign flips---why we read drivers from SHAP,
not raw coefficients. We rejected sequence and transformer architectures: no sequential
structure, low-cardinality categoricals, discrimination data-limited at $\approx\!0.77$ AUC,
and calibration---a scored component---favours the simpler, well-understood stack.

\section{Calibration \& uncertainty quantification}

\emph{We fix the probability \emph{level} on the only data drawn from the scoring window, then
size every interval by measured out-of-sample error rather than in-sample optimism.}

\textbf{Point PD.} Isotonic calibration is \emph{refit on in-window validation} to correct the
$17.5\%{\to}20.6\%$ train-to-window drift (the $a_0{\to}0$ power-prior limit: in-window
likelihood sets the level, history discounted); it stays valid on the approved manifold and is
extrapolated on declines.

\textbf{Intervals (A, C).} Additive on the calibrated point, $p \pm \alpha z \sigma$: the point
probability widened by how much the five folds disagree ($\sigma$), with the multiplier $\alpha$
fit by five-fold \emph{cross-fit} on validation. Validated decile coverage is $\approx 0.90$ at mean width
$\approx 0.06$, tighter than an earlier over-wide ($\approx 100\%$ coverage)
configuration---the brief's ``contain the truth without being needlessly wide.''

\textbf{Trajectory intervals (B).} A within-cohort bootstrap combined with a conformal band
calibrated against the \emph{true} validation cohort trajectories; validated coverage
$\approx 0.92$. Out-of-support counterfactual intervals in C are widened further
(Table~\ref{tab:val}).

\section{Limitations \& what we'd do differently}

\emph{Our honest gaps trace to one fact---we never observe outcomes for declined
applicants---so the decline and counterfactual estimates rest on extrapolation we cannot check.}

\textbf{Self-report and immutable counterfactuals remain heuristic} (confounded proxies use
the estimated $\hat\lambda$ above), and all C effects are unverifiable---the queried applicants
have no labels.

\textbf{Declines cannot be validated.} Approved and declined applicants never overlap (\S1), so
reject inference is infeasible and only RD-style extrapolation remains (deferred); PD on
declines---part of what A is scored on---stays unchecked.

\looseness=-1
\textbf{Timing and cohort corrections lean on the validation window}---they transfer only
because validation and test share 13 weeks, though small per-cohort samples ($n \approx 150$)
add variance. A is also marginally aggressive---a 20\% PD shift still raises P\&L---so a more
conservative level would close the oracle gap.
