\section{Problem framing \& assumptions violated}

We are re-underwriting a historical SMB loan book to maximize realized portfolio
net present value (NPV), not to maximize classification accuracy. Several standard
modelling assumptions fail, and each failure changed our approach.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.70\textwidth]{fig01_prior_score_threshold.png}
\caption{The prior lender's approval rule is a sharp threshold at
$\code{prior\_underwriter\_score}=0.273$. The approved and declined score
distributions do not overlap (left), and the approval rate is a step function of the
score (right); labels are observed only to the right of the cut. This is the source of
the selection bias and the reason inverse-propensity reweighting is infeasible.}
\label{fig:gate}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Labels are missing-not-at-random behind a sharp gate.} Repayment outcomes
exist only for loans the prior lender approved, and approval is, to numerical
precision, $\code{prior\_underwriter\_score} \geq 0.273$: a propensity model
separates approved from declined with AUC $1.00$ and \emph{zero} score overlap.
The training label is therefore selected on a deterministic function of an observed
covariate (Figure~\ref{fig:gate})---textbook sample-selection bias. Crucially, inverse-propensity
reweighting is \emph{infeasible}: a propensity model fit on the ordinary covariates
(no selection features) still reaches AUC $1.00$, so approved and declined
populations do not overlap in feature space and positivity is violated. Only
local extrapolation near the cutoff is available; we treat PD on declined applicants
as honest extrapolation and widen its intervals.

\textbf{The split is forward in time.} Training spans an 18-month book that ends the
day before the 13-week scoring window opens, over which the default rate drifts
$17.5\% \to 20.6\%$. We learn default \emph{timing} on the history and recalibrate
the \emph{level} on the in-window validation set.

\textbf{Missingness is information, not absence.} ``Never declined elsewhere''
(null) loans default at $14.3\%$ versus $21.3\%$ when present; no-bank-feed loans at
$19.1\%$ versus $16.7\%$. We encode these as first-class indicators rather than
imputing them away. \textbf{Self-reports are optimistically biased}: applicants who
overstate revenue by more than $1.5\times$ default at $38.6\%$ versus $16.1\%$. The
\emph{gap} between stated and bank-feed revenue is the signal---central to our
causal treatment of \code{stated\_annual\_revenue} below.

\section{Methodology}

\textbf{One shared discrete-time model.} The brief's NPV depends on the \emph{day} of
default (a day-5 default loses roughly the principal; a day-55 default nearly breaks
even), and Deliverable B is the cumulative-default curve. Both consume a single timing
model. We write each loan's cumulative-default curve as
$F_i(t) = \PD_i \cdot S_{b(i)}(t)$, where $\PD_i = \Pr(y_i{=}1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i)$ is a
calibrated probability of default by day~90 and $S_{b}(t)$ is a normalized timing
shape ($S_b(90){=}1$) learned per credit band $b$.

\textbf{PD model.} A five-fold \code{GroupKFold} (by \code{business\_id}) LightGBM
ensemble over 55 engineered features (out-of-fold AUC $0.774$, Brier $0.117$),
isotonic-calibrated. Features are deliberately buffer-centric: because loans are
$\sim$1.3\% of annual revenue, default is cash-buffer bound rather than revenue bound, so
debt-service coverage, buffer-to-payment, overdrafts and credit utilization dominate---each
a documented function of its declared parents, defensible to a regulator.

\textbf{The timing shape is bimodal and we model it as such} (Figure~\ref{fig:traj}).
Of all defaults, $77.5\%$ are missed-draw events over days $3$--$60$; the interval
$61$--$89$ is empty; the remaining $22.5\%$ are a point mass at day~90 (the open-balance
sweep). A smooth survival fit would smear that mass across weeks; our shape reproduces
the empirical concave-rise/flat/day-90-cliff exactly. The shape is
band-conditional---worse-credit borrowers default $\sim$13 days earlier and carry less
day-90 mass---so $S_b(t)$ feeds both B and the per-loan $\E[t^*]$ used in A.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{fig03_cumulative_trajectory.png}
\caption{Pooled cumulative default rate by loan-age week: a concave rise through
week~9, a flat stretch (weeks 9--12), and the day-90 open-balance cliff at week~13.
Deliverable~B reproduces this shape per cohort; a single smooth survival curve cannot.}
\label{fig:traj}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Deliverable A: decide on the NPV sign.} We approve iff
$\E[\NPV_i \mid \text{approve}] > 0$ using the exact product economics: revenue
$F_i + R_i r (T/365)$ if repaid, and $F_i + D_i(t^*_i - 1) + \mathrm{rec}_i - R_i$ if
default occurs at day $t^*_i$. Because NPV is linear in $t^*$, the expectation uses the
band-conditional expected default day and the empirical recovery fraction ($\approx 9\%$).
This yields a break-even PD of $\approx 0.39$---far above the naive $0.5$ threshold,
because even defaulters repay $\sim$74\% of the schedule through daily ACH before
defaulting. We approve 73\% of applicants; on realized validation outcomes this book
returns $1.74\times$ the prior underwriter's P\&L.

\textbf{Deliverable B: shape $\times$ level.}
$\mathrm{CDR}_{w,a} = \mathrm{mean}_{i \in A_w}\, \PD_i \, S_{b(i)}(a)$ over our approved
cohort $A_w$, monotone by construction. Because the PD model has no cohort signal
(training predates the window), we shrink each cohort's \emph{level} toward the realized
validation rate for the same calendar week (empirical-Bayes, pseudo-count 75); validation
and test span the identical 13 weeks, so this transfers.

\section{Causal reasoning \& counterfactual methodology}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{shap_summary.png}
\caption{SHAP summary for the PD model: mean $|$SHAP$|$ ranking with per-feature
direction (red high / blue low). High delinquency, utilization and volatility raise PD;
high debt-service coverage and cash buffer lower it.}
\label{fig:shap}
\end{figure}

The counterfactual target is
$p_q^{\mathrm{cf}} = \Pr\!\big(y_{i_q}{=}1 \mid \mathrm{do}(f_q{=}v_q),\,
\mathbf{X}_{i_q,-f_q}{=}\mathbf{x}_{i_q,-f_q}\big)$, which differs from the observational
$\Pr(y \mid f{=}v, \mathbf{X}_{-f})$ precisely when $f$ is confounded. Our \textbf{feature
registry} is the consistency layer: each engineered feature declares its parents, so
setting a raw feature and recomputing propagates the intervention to exactly its
descendants and nothing else. For example $\mathrm{do}(\code{requested\_amount})$ flows to
the daily-payment, buffer-to-payment, leverage and ask-to-revenue features while
everything else is held at its observed value; the provided
\code{requested\_amount\_to\_observed\_revenue} is never used, so there is no
stale-descendant bug. This makes our $\mathrm{do}(\cdot)$ a genuine structural
perturbation rather than an unconstrained input edit.

\textbf{We classify the intervenable features by causal status.} \emph{Manipulable
causes}---\code{requested\_amount}, \code{aggregate\_credit\_utilization},
\code{existing\_debt\_obligations}, \code{invoice\_payment\_delinquency\_rate}---are
perturbed and propagated; the model sensitivity is a meaningful interventional effect.
\emph{Self-reports}---\code{stated\_annual\_revenue}, \code{stated\_time\_in\_business}---are
returned at their observational PD, because editing a number on the application does not
change the borrower's type: when the claim does not cause default,
$\Pr(y \mid \mathrm{do}(\text{stated}), \mathbf{X}_{-\text{stated}}) =
\Pr(y \mid \mathbf{X}_{-\text{stated}})$, so a near-zero effect is the causally correct
answer, not naive re-prediction. \emph{Immutable or proxy-by-fiat} features---\code{sector},
\code{geography\_region}, \code{vintage\_years}, \code{has\_linked\_bank\_feed}---are forced
into the query set though they are not manipulable levers; we answer via model perturbation
but shrink the effect and widen the interval, and flag them as proxy effects.

\textbf{Regulator defense.} Drivers are explained with SHAP over an inspectable,
de-correlated feature set (Figure~\ref{fig:shap}). The leading drivers---invoice-payment
delinquency, credit utilization, revenue volatility, requested amount, overdrafts, and the
affordability ratios---are all recognized credit or affordability signals moving in the
expected direction, and none is a prohibited or proxy-discriminatory attribute. The sharp
$0.273$ cutoff is a natural experiment supporting local causal validity. Several queried
values lie outside observed support (e.g.\ \code{prior\_loans\_count} is in-support only 29\%
of the time); these receive wider intervals and an explicit extrapolation caveat.

\textbf{Model-class check.} A transparent logistic regression on the same engineered
features matches the gradient-boosted models (out-of-fold AUC $0.775$ vs $0.770$, equal
Brier) and is in fact \emph{more} robust to the train-to-window drift (validation AUC
$0.759$ vs $0.751$)---evidence that the engineered space is approximately linearly
sufficient and the drivers directly interpretable. Its standardized coefficients agree in
sign with the SHAP ranking and univariate associations on eight of the top ten features;
the two exceptions (\code{buffer\_to\_payment}, \code{debt\_to\_revenue}) are
collinearity-driven partial-coefficient sign flips, which is exactly why we read drivers
from SHAP over the de-correlated set rather than from raw linear coefficients. We evaluated
and rejected sequence and transformer architectures: there is no sequential structure, the
categoricals are low-cardinality, discrimination is data-limited at $\approx\!0.77$ AUC,
and calibration---a scored component---favours the simpler, well-understood stack.

\section{Calibration \& uncertainty quantification}

\textbf{Point PD.} Isotonic calibration is \emph{refit on in-window validation} to correct
the train-to-window drift (training base rate $17.5\%$ versus window $20.6\%$); the caveat
remains that calibration is valid on the approved manifold and extrapolated on declines.

\textbf{Intervals (A, C).} Additive on the calibrated point, $p \pm \alpha z \sigma$, where
$\sigma$ is fold-ensemble disagreement and the width scale $\alpha$ is fit by five-fold
\emph{cross-fit} on validation, so coverage reflects genuine out-of-sample calibration
error rather than collapsing in-sample. Validated decile coverage is $\approx 0.90$ at mean
width $\approx 0.06$, tighter than an earlier over-wide ($\approx 100\%$ coverage)
configuration---the brief's ``contain the truth without being needlessly wide.''

\textbf{Trajectory intervals (B).} A within-cohort bootstrap combined with a conformal band
calibrated against the \emph{true} validation cohort trajectories; validated coverage
$\approx 0.92$. Out-of-support counterfactual intervals in C are widened further.

validation evidence.

\section{Limitations \& what we'd do differently}

\textbf{Counterfactuals on non-causal channels are heuristic.} Self-report and immutable
interventions use shrink/perturbation rather than a learned structural causal model, and C
effects are unverifiable since the queried applicants have no labels.

\textbf{Calibration rests on an approval-selected validation set.} Approval is recoverable
with AUC $\approx 1.0$, so approved and declined populations do not overlap: inverse-propensity
reject inference is infeasible, only RD-style local extrapolation is available (deferred), and
PD on declines---part of what A is scored on---cannot be validated.

\textbf{The timing and cohort corrections lean on the validation window.} They transfer
because validation and test share the identical 13 weeks, but small per-cohort samples
($n \approx 150$) add variance that more in-window data would reduce. Finally, A is marginally
aggressive (a 20\% upward PD shift still raises realized P\&L), so a slightly more conservative
level would close the last of the oracle gap.
